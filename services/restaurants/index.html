Restaurant, Café & Bar Build-Outs in Santa Cruz

Restaurant construction is its own discipline. Type I hoods, grease interceptors, Ansul systems, three-compartment sinks, hand sinks every eight feet, FRP walls, coved base, quarry tile — get any one of those wrong and the health inspector walks out without signing. What makes a restaurant build-out different

You're coordinating Santa Cruz County Environmental Health, the fire marshal, the building department, and sometimes the ABC — plus your equipment vendors, POS installer, and a chef who needs the layout to actually work during service. We run that coordination:

  • Type I commercial hood — sized to equipment, UL-listed, ducted to exterior with welded seams, with make-up air balanced so the room isn't pulling negative when the line is firing
  • Ansul (or equivalent) fire suppression — designed, installed, and certified; tied into the shunt-trip and fire alarm
  • Grease interceptor — sized per the County's flow-rate formula, installed with access, plumbed so every fixture that can produce grease actually drains through it
  • Three-compartment sink + dedicated hand sinks + mop sink — all with the right air gaps, indirect waste where required, and backflow preventers
  • FRP or stainless wall panels + coved base + quarry tile or sealed concrete floor — every transition sealed; no harborage for contamination
  • Walk-in cooler and freezer — slab detail (usually depressed to get a flush entry), refrigeration lines, condensate routing, condenser placement
  • Dedicated HVAC — balanced with hood make-up air, properly filtered supply, separate return paths for kitchen vs. dining
  • Grease-duct fire rating — two-hour shaft wrap where the duct passes through occupied space, inspection access doors at required intervals

Front of house

Beyond the kitchen, the dining experience is what brings guests back. We do:

  • Custom millwork — bar tops, hostess stands, banquettes, shelving
  • Sound treatment — acoustic ceiling clouds, fabric panels, soft seating that keeps a full room conversational
  • Lighting design — task lighting at every station, warm ambient over dining, dimmable zones
  • Restrooms — fully ADA-compliant with health-department-acceptable finishes
  • Outdoor patios and parklets where zoning allows — permitted, lit, and heated for year-round use

Timelines and budgets

  • Small café / coffee shop buildout (1,000–1,500 sq ft): $400,000–$800,000 / 12–18 weeks
  • Full-service restaurant (2,500–4,000 sq ft): $900,000–$2.2M / 16–28 weeks
  • Bar / tasting room: $350,000–$900,000 / 10–16 weeks
  • Kitchen-only refresh with new hood and equipment: $150,000–$450,000 / 6–10 weeks

Equipment lead times drive schedule more than construction does. Hoods, walk-ins, and cooking lines can run 8–14 weeks. We order early so the gear lands when the rough-ins are ready.

Working with Environmental Health

Santa Cruz County Environmental Health plan-checks every restaurant and has an inspector walk the space before opening. We know their requirements line by line and we build to them the first time — no reopening walls after inspection. We've never had a restaurant fail its final Environmental Health inspection.

FAQ

Common questions about restaurant

We signed a lease. How fast can we open?

For a standard buildout in a space that already had restaurant use: 4–6 months from signed contract to doors open. For a conversion from retail or office to a new restaurant, plan on 6–9 months because of the Environmental Health plan check and utility upgrades.

Do you handle the equipment package?

We coordinate closely with the equipment vendor you choose (or recommend one). We handle rough-ins, final connections, hood and Ansul install, and certification. You own the equipment contract so warranties stay clean.

Can you do a ghost-kitchen or commissary buildout?

Yes — we've built production kitchens for catering and delivery-only concepts. Same code path as a restaurant kitchen, just without the front of house.

What about ABC (liquor license) requirements?

We build to what ABC needs — proper dispensing areas, minor-entry restrictions for Type 48 bars, required signage locations. The license application itself is your attorney's or consultant's job, but we make sure the space passes the ABC walkthrough.
